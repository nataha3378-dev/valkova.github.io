01Context & positioning

Not a tool for the director. A tool for the producer.

The market leaders — StudioBinder, Yamdu, Celtx — are built for film: script → breakdown → stripboard → call sheet. Heavy, "from-the-screenplay" instruments.

But commercial production — ad shoots, brand content, events, photo — has a different pain. Not "how do I break down a script," but "how do I run many client projects in parallel without losing money, people and gear."

CREW OS is positioned as the studio's operating system: the place where projects, the team, equipment, estimates, approvals and client access all live. Software for the producer and the production manager who hold the business together.

02Problem & hypothesis

Today

The producer lives in Excel + Google Sheets + messengers. An operator gets double-booked across two shoots. The budget quietly drifts from the plan. A client's rejection is buried three chat threads deep. Nothing is a system — everything is a copy-paste.

Hypothesis

A single operational surface — with multi-project resource management and conflict detection, real-time plan-vs-actual budgets, and versioned client approvals — turns that chaos into a system the studio can actually run on.

03Roles & permissions

One project screen looks different to seven roles.

From Owner to Client, everyone touches the same project — but sees a different slice of it. So RBAC is designed as a system, not as "hide a button."

The matrix maps entity × role × permission — view / comment / edit / approve / no-access — across 12 entities and 7 roles. Cells are editable; the same budget line renders four different ways depending on who's looking, down to a real 403 state.

  • Owner / Admin — the whole studio
  • Producer — own projects, end to end
  • Coordinator, Dept head — scoped down
  • Freelancer, Vendor — only their part
  • Client — the portal, nothing internal
04Architecture & platform strategy

Web is the HQ.
Mobile is the field.

Web (desktop) is where the heavy work happens — planning, estimates, resources, dense tables, approvals, analytics. The producer spends the day here.

Mobile is the shoot day — see your call sheet, confirm attendance, snap the set, mark "en route / on set." Minimum input, maximum "glanced → got it → confirmed."

The principle that ties them: same data, a different slice per context. A shift is a row in the web timeline — and a "today" card on the phone. One object, two representations.

05Key UX decisions

Four problems, four systemic answers.

ProblemSolution
ProblemPeople and gear are shared across projects — and get double-booked with no warning.
SolutionA shared resource pool with an occupancy timeline that detects overlaps and offers a fix: reschedule the shift, find a replacement, or request from the other project's producer. Overlap days are hatched red; each conflict card shows the exact clashing interval.
ProblemBudgets drift from the plan and no one notices until it's too late.
SolutionA dense estimate → committed → actual table with variance highlighted per line, grouped by department, with drill-down to why a line went over — tied back to the very schedule conflict that caused it.
ProblemClient approvals get lost in chat; nobody knows which version was signed off.
SolutionA versioned approval flow — draft → sent → in review → changes → approved — with a diff that shows exactly what changed between v1 and v2, and a threaded discussion pinned to the line in question.
ProblemCall sheets are static PDFs — no one knows who actually got it or confirmed.
SolutionA call sheet with a builder/preview toggle, personal call times per crew member, and live delivery & confirmation status — 9 of 14 confirmed, one declined and flagged for replacement.

09Reflection

What the case set out to prove

That a B2B product can carry genuinely complex logic — multi-role permissions, dense data, live states, versioned approvals, plan-vs-actual — and still stay legible. The through-line helps: one story (a gaffer's extra day) travels from a resource conflict, into a budget overrun, into an approval diff, to the client, onto a call sheet, and back to the coordinator's dashboard.

Where I'd push further

  • Real finance visualization on the studio level (cashflow, P&L per project)
  • An offline field mode — shoots happen in basements and forests
  • A designed notification system across web and mobile
  • Template libraries for estimates and call sheets
