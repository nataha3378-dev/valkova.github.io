01
Context & market

Everyone owns the warehouse. Nobody owns the trust.

Furniture rental is growing fast — roughly 7–12% CAGR — pulled by urban mobility, renting over buying, a Gen-Z shift away from ownership, and the circular-economy mood. Around 60% of bookings already happen on mobile.

But the established players — Feather, Furlenco, CORT, Rentomojo — all run the same warehouse model: the company owns the inventory and rents it out. The peer-to-peer segment — people renting their own designer pieces to each other, Airbnb-for-furniture — is almost empty.

That gap is the opportunity. It's also where the real design problem lives: a two-sided flow, physical condition that changes hands, and money held in the middle.

02
Problem & hypothesis

High-value objects, two strangers, no company to trust.

Renting from a company means trusting one party — the brand. P2P removes that anchor: now it's two people, a €2,000 chair, and a return date. The object's condition changes hands, a deposit sits in limbo, and a single dispute can poison the whole transaction.

Hypothesis — if the platform makes trust legible (evidence, escrow, clear status, honest disclosure), two strangers will hand an expensive piece back and forth without a single phone call.

Owning inventory is a supply problem.
Owning trust is a design problem.

03
Audience & roles

One account, two modes.

Like Airbnb, a single person is both sides of the market. That duality drives the navigation and the whole component model — the same booking object has to read clearly from two opposite points of view.

Renter

Temporary living · stylists and photographers shooting for a few days · event managers · "try the expensive chair before I buy it."

Lender

Collectors and design enthusiasts with idle pieces · vintage sellers and small showrooms · people downsizing who'd rather lend than sell.

The platform is the silent third party — escrow, deposit, insurance, and mediation — present everywhere as badges and status, never as a screen of its own.

04
Information architecture

Lending isn't a second app. It's a layer.

Five tabs carry the renter experience. Switching to lending doesn't open a different product — it re-skins the context: Rentals becomes Listings, and the lender dashboard surfaces. One identity, two modes, one mental model.

The full architecture maps ~38 screens across onboarding, catalogue, booking, active rental, condition, lending, and system. Ten were taken to hi-fi — one complete happy path and one sad path — with the rest defined at IA level.

05
Key UX decisions

Three hard problems, three deliberate answers.

Challenge · the centerpiece

The two-sided flow

How do you let one person search and lend without shipping two apps? The booking is a single object that must read perfectly from both ends — a request to the renter is an inbox decision to the lender.

Solution — a mode switch on the profile re-skins the whole context. The same booking renders as a price summary for the renter and an accept/decline card for the lender. One component, two truths.

Challenge · the trust core

Condition, without the conflict

In P2P the scariest moment is "you damaged it." Whose word counts? The UX has to defuse that before it happens.

Solution — a single trust loop. The lender discloses flaws honestly when listing → the renter does a timestamped photo check-in on arrival → a claim is settled by before/after evidence in a deliberately neutral tone. Facts, not fault.

Challenge · the money

Make the deposit make sense

Rental plus service fee plus deposit plus delivery plus insurance — five numbers, one anxious tap. And "when do I get my €300 back?" is the question that kills conversion.

Solution — a transparent, reusable price breakdown, and a deposit that's authorized, not charged — held in escrow, released 48h after return, shown as a tiny timeline so the money's path is never a mystery.

06
Design system

A system, not a pile of screens.

The brief was systems thinking, so the visual language is tight on purpose: warm-neutral surfaces, near-black ink, one warm-coral accent, and Hanken Grotesk doing all the work from 12px captions to 54px stat figures. Bento tiles carry the catalogue; everything else is a small, repeatable kit.

09
Reflection

What the case shows

  • Systems thinking — one status language, one booking object, two role views.
  • Complex product logic — escrow, deposits, buffers, a claim flow — handled as UX, not legalese.
  • A point of view on trust — the differentiator isn't a feature, it's a loop across screens.

What I'd push further

  • AR at scale — "see it in your room" as a real, tested feature, not a concept.
  • Claim mediation — the deeper resolution and appeal flow.
  • Onboarding & verification — the first-run trust-building, and a full motion spec.
